03 — Mastering the Scopes

Emissions are categorized into three "Scopes" based on who owns the source and where the heat is released. Understanding this boundary logic is the most critical skill for a carbon accountant.

Scope 1

Direct Emissions

Sources you **own or control**. Boilers, furnaces, and company-owned vehicles.

Scope 2

Energy Indirect

Emissions from **purchased electricity**, steam, heating, or cooling.

Scope 3

The Heavyweight

Value Chain Indirect

Everything else in your **upstream and downstream** value chain.

category

The Scope Sorter

"Burning natural gas in a building owned by the company."

Strategic Insight

Why is Spend-Based data considered a "Transition Tool"?

Spend-based data allows companies to quickly map their "hotspots," but because market prices fluctuate, it cannot provide the granular accuracy needed for reduction tracking. Use it to start, but transition to activity-based data to lead.

05 — The Emission Factor Lab

An Emission Factor (EF) is the "exchange rate" for carbon. It converts a physical activity (like liters of diesel) into CO2-equivalent.

Why Factors
Change?

Emission factors for electricity change yearly based on the "Grid Mix" (how much wind vs coal). Factors for fuel change based on refining intensity.

Always use the most recent, geo-specific factor for audit-ready results.

The Net Zero Target

To limit warming to 1.5°C, global emissions must reach "Net Zero" by 2050. This means reducing emissions as much as possible and removing any remaining carbon from the atmosphere.
