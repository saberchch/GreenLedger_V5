01 The Alphabet Soup

The world of ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) reporting is notorious for its acronyms. Click the cards below to decode the major frameworks dictating global carbon disclosures.

public

CDP

Voluntary

Carbon Disclosure Project

The original global disclosure system. Companies submit massive questionnaires, and CDP grades them from A to D-. Used heavily by large investors to judge risk.

account_balance

TCFD

Strategic

Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures

Created by the Financial Stability Board. Focuses purely on financial risk: How will climate change (floods, carbon taxes) bankrupt your business?

gavel

CSRD

Mandatory Law (EU)

Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive

The game changer. European law requiring ~50,000 companies to file sustainability reports alongside their financial reports, audited by third parties.

Applies to:

  • • >250 Employees
  • • >€50M Turnover
  • • >€25M Balance Sheet

03 The Annual Lifecycle

Carbon accounting is highly cyclical, mirroring the financial reporting year.

Q1

Collection

Aggregating utility bills, spend data, and supplier inputs from the previous calendar year.

Q2

Calculation & Audit

Engine processes data. Third-party auditors (Big 4) verify the logic and evidence documentation.

Q3

Disclosure

Final numbers are published to CDP, government regulators, and included in the public Annual Report.

04 Deep Dive: Double Materiality

Under the EU's CSRD, identifying what to report is based on "Double Materiality". It asks two distinct questions.

payments

1. Financial Materiality

"Outside-In" Perspective

How does climate change impact the company's financial value?

Example: A beverage company realizing that droughts will destroy its supply chain and profits.

public

2. Impact Materiality

"Inside-Out" Perspective

How does the company's existence impact the global climate and society?

Example: The same company calculating the massive CO2 emissions from its plastic bottling factories.

05 Target Setting (SBTi)

Not just "Net Zero"

Corporate pledges to be "Net Zero by 2050" are often meaningless marketing if they rely entirely on buying cheap carbon offsets (planting trees) rather than actually reducing emissions.

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) acts as the global referee. They evaluate a company's reduction glidepath to ensure it mathematically aligns with the Paris Agreement goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C.

check_circle SBTi Approved Target

"Company X commits to reduce absolute Scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions 42% by 2030 from a 2020 base year, and reduce Scope 3 emissions 25% within the same timeframe."
