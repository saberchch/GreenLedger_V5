Scope 1 Accounting

The Direct Perimeter

Deep-dive into accounting for direct emissions. Master the four pillars: stationary combustion, mobile combustion, fugitive emissions, and process emissions.

01 The Direct Perimeter

Scope 1 encompasses all direct GHG emissions originating from sources that are owned or controlled by your organization. If you literally burn the fuel, or if the refrigerant leaks from equipment you own, it is Scope 1.

Mobile & Fugitive

Automobiles, trucks, ships, airplanes, refrigerant leaks, wastewater treatment, and cooling towers.

02 Stationary Combustion

Combustion of fuels in stationary equipment. The most common pitfall here is mismatching the units of your activity data with your emission factor.

The Calorific Reality Check

local_fire_department

GCV (Gross or Higher)

Includes the latent heat of vaporization of water in the combustion products. Used primarily in the US and UK.

Natural Gas GCV EF: 0.183 kgCO2e / kWh

NCV (Net or Lower)

Excludes latent heat. Standard across most of the world and international frameworks (e.g., IPCC, EU ETS).

Natural Gas NCV EF: 0.202 kgCO2e / kWh

Rule of thumb: NCV is ~10% lower than GCV for natural gas. If you use a GCV factor on NCV data, you will under-report your emissions by ~10%.

04 Fugitive Emissions

Intentional or unintentional releases of GHGs. The most common and impactful are hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) from refrigeration and air conditioning equipment.

The Asymmetric Impact of Refrigerants

A single kilogram of leaked refrigerant can wipe out years of energy efficiency initiatives due to their massive Global Warming Potential (GWP).

1 kg of R-410A (AC unit leak) ≈ 2,088 kg CO₂e
1 kg of SF₆ (Electrical switchgear) ≈ 23,500 kg CO₂e
1 kg of CO₂ 1 kg CO₂e

05 Process Direct Emissions

Emissions from physical or chemical processes. For most service and technology companies, this is zero. If you manufacture cement, steel, or aluminum, this might be your largest emission source.

science

The Calcination Process

In cement manufacturing, heating limestone (CaCO₃) releases CO₂ to form lime (CaO). This is a purely chemical release, separate from the fuel burned to heat the kiln.
