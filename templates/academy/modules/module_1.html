Module 01: Climate Change Fundamentals

This module introduces the scientific foundations of climate change and explains why greenhouse gas accounting is necessary for organizations and governments.

1 — Earth Energy Balance

The climate system of Earth is governed by a delicate balance between the energy received from the Sun and the energy that the planet radiates back into space. This equilibrium determines the average temperature of our planet and allowed life to flourish for millennia.

Hover over the energy streams to see how Earth maintains its thermal balance. In a stable climate, Energy In = Energy Out.

Quick Check

What happens if the energy leaving Earth is less than the energy entering?

Analyze the Result expand_more
The surplus energy is stored as heat, primarily in the oceans. This "thermal inertia" leads to a gradual increase in global average temperatures, disrupting the stable climate patterns life depends on.

2 — The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms the Earth's surface. After solar radiation is absorbed by the planet, the Earth emits heat in the form of infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases acting like a Thermal Blanket absorb part of this heat and re-emit it back toward the surface.

ac_unit

No Atmosphere

≈ -18°C

Without the greenhouse effect, Earth would be a frozen wasteland, too cold to support liquid water or complex life.

thermostat

Natural Greenhouse

≈ +15°C

The balance of natural gases creates the perfect environment for ecosystems to thrive. This is our baseline.

The Mechanism of Action

1

Capture

Solar rays penetrate the atmosphere and warm the surface.

2

Transform

The warm surface emits long-wave infrared radiation.

3

Trap

GHG molecules absorb this heat instead of letting it escape.

4

Reflect

Heat is re-emitted in all directions, warming the lower atmosphere.

verified

Knowledge Check

Is the greenhouse effect inherently bad?

Reveal Scientific Consensus expand_more
No. The greenhouse effect is essential for life. The issue is the "Enhanced Greenhouse Effect" caused by human activity, which thickens the atmospheric blanket beyond natural levels, leading to global warming.

3 — Anthropogenic Climate Change

While the Earth's climate has always experienced natural variations, the rapid warming observed since the mid-20th century is primarily the result of human activities. This period is often called the Anthropocene.

local_fire_department

Energy Production

Burning coal, oil, and gas for electricity and heat remains the largest source of global CO₂ emissions.

factory

Industrial Output

Chemical transformations in cement, steel, and chemical manufacturing release GHGs directly.

agriculture

Agri-Emissions

Livestock, fertilizers, and deforestation release massive amounts of Methane and Nitrous Oxide.

Landfills and wastewater produce Methane during organic decomposition in anaerobic conditions.

Carbon accounting exists to map these human activities. By quantifying impact, we can manage the transition back to a stable energy balance.

4 — Greenhouse Gas Families

The Kyoto Protocol identifies six primary greenhouse gases that are the focus of international carbon accounting. Each gas has a unique chemical structure and warming potential.

cloud CO₂

Carbon Dioxide

The primary benchmark gas. Produced by fossil fuel combustion and deforestation.

agriculture CH₄

Methane

Shorter-lived but much more potent than CO₂. Emitted from livestock and landfills.

Released during fertilizer application and industrial combustion processes.

The fluorinated "F-Gases"

HFCs, PFCs, and SF₆ are human-made gases used in refrigeration and electronics. While their volume is small, their Global Warming Potential is thousands of times higher than CO₂.

5 — Global Warming Potential (GWP)

Since different gases trap heat with varying efficiency, we use GWP to normalize their climate impact over a 100-year horizon.

The Potency Scale

CO₂ (Reference) GWP: 1
Methane (CH₄) GWP: 28
Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) GWP: 265
SF₆ (Maximum Impact) GWP: 23,500

Note: Values based on IPCC AR5 100-year time horizon. Progress bars represent relative impact visibility.

Critical Concept

Why do we use the 100-year GWP specifically?

Read Scientific Note expand_more
While some gases breakdown quickly (Methane in ~12 years), they have an intense immediate impact. International standards use the 100-year window to balance these short-term shocks with the long-term persistence of CO₂ (which stays in the atmosphere for centuries).

Measuring carbon isn't just about compliance — it's about business resilience. In a net-zero economy, carbon is a new financial metric.
